Department for the Blind and Vision Impaired

For students who are visually impaired, getting around campus can be a challenge. The Virginia Department for the Blind and Vision Impaired (DBVI) allows students with this sensory disability to learn how to get around campus and help them better succeed in the classroom.

Students with vision impairment can be paired with an Orientation Mobility Specialist to help them better learn their environment. Steven Ferguson, the Orientation and Mobility Specialist for James Madison University, works with students to guide them to not only their classrooms when needed but to areas in their community including Downtown Harrisonburg.

Other services DBVI offers include assisted technology to help that can include a closed circuit television, a portable device that magnifies text, and laptop with software that enlarges the screen.

The Office of Disability Services

The Office of Disability Services (ODS) works with students who have disabilities to accommodate their individual needs. It has existed in a variety of capacities for about 30 years, and was officially mandated by law when the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 was passed. Currently, the office provides services to over 1,000 students. According to their website, ODS’ mission is to “collaborate with the JMU community providing programs and services that support the university in creating inclusive, equitable environments that value disability, diversity and accessibility.”

Over the past 10 years, the Office of Disability Services has grown in staff from six to 42. In 2010, ODS became its own department. Previously, it was a part of the Learning Center. It was also relocated in the fall of 2014 from Wilson Hall to the new Student Success Center. Because of growth in office space after the move, ODS is able to accommodate a larger number of students.

The Office of Disability Services offers a variety of different resources and services including learning strategies, note takers, and accessible media. For visually impaired students specifically, ODS provides brail serves where textbooks, tests, and class materials can be printed. For the hearing impaired, ODS has audio technology where video materials that may be used for classes can be captioned. They also translate tests to audio format and provide interpreters for campus events.

The Office of Disability Services at JMU also sponsors Disability Awareness Week to educate the community about disability and the services provided by ODS, as well as to bring disability into the mainstream. ODS’ 8th annual Disability Awareness Week was March 27-31, 2017. It included speakers, a variety of modified sporting events, and faculty training on accessible Microsoft materials.

On Tuesday, March 28, a Disabled Sports Showcase was co-sponsored by UREC, the Department of Kinesiology, HART School of Hospitality, and Sport and Recreation Management. Students from the university were invited to come learn about and participate in modified sports such as beep baseball. In this game students wore a blindfold while playing baseball. The equipment beeped, using certain tones to signify before it had been hit and when it was in the field. Bases also beeped for blindfolded batters to find the bases using senses other than sight. Students were also able to experience walking blindfolded with a walking stick, relying on other senses and the instruction of their peers to maneuver around obstacles.
